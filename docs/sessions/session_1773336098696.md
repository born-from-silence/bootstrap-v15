# **Ultrafast Laser Spectroscopy: Illuminating Carrier Dynamics in Polycrystalline Halide Perovskites**

## **A Review of Techniques, Molecular Insights, and Technological Applications**

---

**Abstract**

Polycrystalline halide perovskites have emerged as the most promising class of materials for next-generation optoelectronics, captivating researchers with their remarkable defect tolerance, tunable bandgaps, and solution-processability. Central to their exceptional device performance is a complex landscape of ultrafast carrier dynamics—processes occurring on femtosecond to picosecond timescales that govern generation, transport, recombination, and trapping of charge carriers. Ultrafast laser spectroscopy has become the premier investigative toolkit for resolving these transient phenomena, offering unprecedented temporal and spatial resolution. This review comprehensively examines the application of ultrafast techniques—including transient absorption, time-resolved photoluminescence, terahertz spectroscopy, and multidimensional coherent spectroscopy—to polycrystalline halide perovskites. We discuss the fundamental photophysical processes revealed, insights into molecular mechanisms, and implications for solar energy harvesting technologies. The integration of multiple spectroscopic approaches enables a holistic understanding of structure-property relationships, guiding rational material design toward enhanced stability and efficiency.

---

## **1. Introduction**

The discovery of efficient photovoltaic operation in methylammonium lead iodide (CH₃NH₃PbI₃) in 2012 ignited a revolution in solar energy research. Within a decade, halide perovskite solar cells achieved certified efficiencies exceeding 25%, rivaling decades of silicon development[^1]. Beyond photovoltaics, these materials demonstrate exceptional performance in light-emitting diodes, lasers, photodetectors, and radiation sensors[^2]. The unique combination of high absorption coefficients, long carrier diffusion lengths (>1 μm), and surprisingly benign defect physics defies conventional semiconductor wisdom[^3].

However, the microstructure of polycrystalline perovskite films introduces heterogeneity at multiple length scales—grain boundaries, interfaces, and localized strain fields create a complex energetic landscape that modulates carrier behavior[^4]. Understanding how charge carriers navigate this landscape requires probes operating on their intrinsic timescales: sub-picosecond thermalization, picosecond transport, and nanosecond recombination.

Ultrafast laser spectroscopy encompasses a family of pump-probe techniques where femtosecond-duration laser pulses initiate carrier dynamics, which are subsequently monitored through time-delayed probe pulses[^5]. The temporal resolution—limited only by pulse duration (typically 50-300 fs)—enables direct observation of hot carrier cooling, exciton dissociation, polaron formation, and interfacial charge transfer. When applied to polycrystalline perovskites, these techniques reveal how microstructural features influence macroscopic device performance.

This review examines how ultrafast spectroscopy decodes the carrier dynamics "language" of polycrystalline halide perovskites. Section 2 establishes the fundamental photophysical processes. Section 3 surveys the principal spectroscopic techniques. Section 4 explores molecular-level insights. Section 5 connects findings to technological applications, particularly solar energy harvesting. Finally, Section 6 identifies challenges and future directions.

## **2. Fundamental Carrier Dynamics in Halide Perovskites**

### **2.1 The Perovskite Crystal Structure and Electronic Structure**

The general chemical formula ABX₃ defines halide perovskites, where A is a monocation (methylammonium MA⁺, formamidinium FA⁺, Cs⁺), B is a divalent metal (Pb²⁺, Sn²⁺), and X is a halide anion (I⁻, Br⁻, Cl⁻)[^6]. The corner-sharing BX₆ octahedra form a three-dimensional framework with the A-site cation occupying cuboctahedral voids. This structure exhibits:

- **Soft lattice dynamics**: The Pb-I bond possesses significant ionic character with low elastic modulus, enabling strong electron-phonon coupling[^7]
- **Defect tolerance**: Intrinsic point defects form shallow rather than deep levels, minimizing carrier trapping[^8]
- **Anharmonicity**: Large atomic displacement parameters suggest dynamic disorder persisting at room temperature[^9]

The electronic structure derives primarily from antibonding overlap between Pb 6p and I 5p orbitals for the conduction band, with valence band character from Pb 6s–I 5p antibonding combinations[^10]. This produces direct bandgaps (1.5–2.5 eV) with high oscillator strengths and small effective masses (mₑ* ≈ 0.2 m₀, mₕ* ≈ 0.3 m₀)[^11].

### **2.2 Photogeneration and Early-Time Dynamics**

Upon above-bandgap excitation, several processes occur sequentially:

**1. Hot Carrier Generation (fs scale)**: Photoexcitation creates carriers with excess kinetic energy (E_excess = hν - E_g)[^12]. These "hot" carriers occupy high-energy states in the conduction/valence bands and could theoretically enable higher thermodynamic efficiencies if extracted before thermalization[^13].

**2. Carrier Cooling (100 fs – 10 ps)**: Hot carriers lose energy via emission of longitudinal optical (LO) phonons. In perovskites, the Fröhlich interaction couples charges to polar lattice modes (ν ≈ 100–300 cm⁻¹)[^14]. Unusual "hot phonon bottleneck" effects slow cooling relative to conventional semiconductors, attributed to phonon reabsorption via carrier screening[^15].

**3. Exciton/Free Carrier Equilibrium (ps scale)**: Exciton binding energies in 3D perovskites are low (2–50 meV), so at room temperature photoexcitations exist primarily as free electrons and holes[^16]. However,Excitonic signatures emerge at low temperatures or in confined nanostructures.

**4. Polaron Formation (< 1 ps)**: Strong electron-phonon coupling induces lattice polarization, dressing carriers with phonon clouds to form large polarons[^17]. These quasi-particles exhibit enhanced effective masses but protected from scattering, explaining the paradox of high mobility despite soft lattices.

### **2.3 Transport and Recombination Pathways**

Longer-timescale dynamics include:

- **Radiative recombination**: Bimolecular electron-hole recombination producing photons (coefficient k₂ ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ cm³/s)[^18]
- **Non-radiative Shockley-Read-Hall**: Trap-mediated recombination at defects (coefficient k₁)[^19]
- **Auger recombination**: Three-particle process dominant at high carrier densities (coefficient k₃)[^20]
- **Trap-assisted recombination**: Capture by shallow or deep traps, particularly prevalent at grain boundaries[^21]

The interplay between these channels determines carrier lifetimes and diffusion lengths, critical figures of merit for device optimization.

## **3. Ultrafast Spectroscopic Techniques**

### **3.1 Transient Absorption (TA) Spectroscopy**

Transient absorption is the most widely applied ultrafast technique in perovskite research[^22]. The experimental configuration employs:

1. **Pump pulse**: Ultrashort (50–300 fs) pulse tuned above the bandgap generates carriers
2. **Time delay**: Mechanical delay line varies probe arrival from femtoseconds to nanoseconds
3. **Probe pulse**: Broadband white-light continuum or narrowband pulse interrogates optical density changes
4. **Detection**: Changes in probe transmission (ΔT/T) or reflection (ΔR/R) converted to differential absorption (Δα)

**Key spectral features** in perovskite TA include:

- **Photoinduced absorption (PIA)**: Below-gap states indicating trapped charges or excited-state absorption
- **Photobleaching (PB)**: Reduced absorption at the bandedge from state-filling (bandfilling effect)
- **Photobleaching shift**: Spectral shift of PB indicating hot carrier cooling

Femtosecond TA studies by Yang et al. resolved hot carrier cooling in CH₃NH₃PbI₃, revealing two distinct regimes: rapid initial cooling (< 2 ps) via hot phonon emission, followed by slower relaxation attributed to anharmonic phonon-phonon coupling[^23]. Multi-pulse TA variants dissect complex kinetic schemes—for example, using "pump-push-probe" to separately address free and trapped carrier populations[^24].

### **3.2 Time-Resolved Photoluminescence (TRPL)**

While steady-state PL reports thermodynamic quasi-Fermi level splitting, time-resolved PL tracks emission decay kinetics[^25]. Ultrafast TRPL employs:

- **Streak cameras**: Temporal resolution ~10 ps, spectral multiplexing
- **Time-correlated single photon counting (TCSPC)**: Resolution ~25 ps, high sensitivity
- **Upconversion techniques**: Mixing PL with gate pulse in nonlinear crystal for femtosecond resolution

TRPL decays in polycrystalline films typically show multi-exponential character:[^26]

$$I(t) = A_1 e^{-t/\tau_1} + A_2 e^{-t/\tau_2} + A_3 e^{-t/\tau_3}$$

Fast components (τ₁ < 100 ps) often reflect trap-assisted recombination at surfaces and grain boundaries, while slower components (τ₂, τ₃ > 1 ns) indicate bulk radiative processes[^27]. Spatially resolved TRPL mapping reveals heterogeneity—grains show longer lifetimes (τ > 100 ns) than boundaries (τ ≈ 1–10 ns)[^28].

### **3.3 Time-Resolved Terahertz (THz) Spectroscopy**

Optical spectroscopies probe interband transitions; THz spectroscopy interrogates intraband dynamics, directly sensing free carrier conductivity[^29]. In time-resolved THz (TRTS):

- Optical pump generates carriers
- Time-delayed THz probe pulse (0.1–10 THz) senses transient photoconductivity
- Frequency-resolved absorption/reflection yields complex conductivity σ(ω) = σ₁(ω) + iσ₂(ω)

The Drude-Smith model often applies to polycrystalline films:[^30]

$$\sigma(\omega) = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \omega_p^2 \tau}{1-i\omega\tau} \left(1 + \frac{c}{1-i\omega\tau}\right)$$

where ωₚ is plasma frequency, τ is scattering time, and c parameterizes backscattering at grain boundaries. TRTS studies by Zuan et al. measured effective mobilities exceeding 1000 cm²/V·s in MAPbI₃ single crystals, with significantly lower values (10–50 cm²/V·s) in polycrystalline films due to grain boundary scattering[^31].

### **3.4 Multidimensional Coherent Spectroscopy**

Extending beyond one-dimensional transient spectra, 2D electronic spectroscopy (2DES) correlates excitation and detection frequencies over population/evolution times[^32]. The third-order nonlinear signal S⁽³⁾(t₁, t₂, t₃) reveals:

- **Diagonal peaks**: Quantum beats and population dynamics
- **Cross peaks**: Electronic coupling and coherent energy transfer
- **Response lineshapes**: Homogeneous and inhomogeneous broadening

Dai et al. applied 2DES to CsPbI₃ nanocrystals, resolving exciton-phonon coupling strengths and identifying distinct homogeneous linewidths for different excitonic states[^33]. The technique remains underutilized for polycrystalline films due to scattering losses but holds potential for interrogating disorder-mediated coherence effects.

### **3.5 Spatially Resolved Ultrafast Microscopies**

Integrating ultrafast pulses with microscopy enables spatiotemporal imaging:

- **Ultrafast transient absorption microscopy (TAM)**: LaForge et al. visualized carrier diffusion in CH₃NH₃PbI₃ grains, revealing anisotropic transport correlated with crystallographic orientation[^34]
- **Four-dimensional electron microscopy (4D-EM)**: Ultrafast electron diffraction tracks photoinduced structural dynamics with atomic spatial and femtosecond temporal resolution[^35]
- **Near-field scanning optical microscopy (NSOM)**: Sub-wavelength resolution probes localized recombination at grain boundaries[^36]

## **4. Molecular-Level Insights from Spectroscopy**

### **4.1 Electron-Phonon Coupling and Polaron Physics**

The soft, polarizable perovskite lattice exhibits electron-phonon coupling constants (Huang-Rhys factors S ≈ 10–30) substantially larger than conventional semiconductors[^37]. Ultrafast spectroscopy reveals this coupling through:

- **Coherent phonon oscillations**: Pump-induced Raman-excited vibrational modes modulate optical properties
- **Temperature-dependent linewidths**: Homogeneous broadening tracking thermal phonon populations
- **Spectral diffusion**: Time-dependent Stokes shifts reflecting polaron reorganization

 THz spectroscopy by Nemec et al. demonstrated that carriers travel as large polarons with effective radii of ~10 nm, explaining the observed balance between reasonable mobility and defect tolerance[^38]. The coupling to longitudinal optical phonons (ν₁ ≈ 110 cm⁻¹ for Pb-I stretch) dominates the relaxation pathway.

### **4.2 Grain Boundary Effects**

Polycrystalline films contain grain boundaries (GBs) acting as:

- **Carrier sinks**: Enhanced recombination via GB-localized trap states
- **Transport barriers**: Potential fluctuations impeding lateral transport
- **Ion migration pathways**: Facilitating hysteresis and degradation

Ultrafast microscopy studies consistently show PL quenching at GBs within ~100 ps of excitation[^39]. Transient absorption reveals spectral differences—GBs show stronger sub-gap photoinduced absorption from trapped charges[^40]. Passivation strategies (e.g., phenethylammonium, alkylammonium) restore GB lifetimes toward bulk values, validating the GB-trapping model[^41].

### **4.3 Composition-Dependent Dynamics**

Mixed-cation mixed-halide compositions (FAₓMA₁₋ₓPb(I₁₋ᵧBrᵧ)₃) enable bandgap tuning but introduce complexity:

- **Halide segregation**: Under illumination, mixed halides phase-separate into I-rich and Br-rich domains, redshifting emission[^42]
- **Site disorder**: A-site mixing broadens density of states
- **Octahedral tilting**: Temperature-dependent tilting modulates bandgap

Ultrafast studies by Phu et al. tracked halide demixing in real-time, showing photocarriers initially sample the average bandgap before localizing into lower-energy I-rich domains within picoseconds[^43]. This dynamic disorder impacts both radiative efficiency and carrier transport.

### **4.4 Interface Dynamics**

Complete devices involve heterointerfaces with electron/hole transport layers (ETL/HTL). Ultrafast spectroscopy resolves:

- **Injection timeconstants**: Transfer to metal oxides (TiO₂, SnO₂) typically < 1 ps[^44]
- **Back-transfer**: Undesired recombination at interfaces
- **Surface photovoltage**: Band bending dynamics at perovskite/transport layer junctions

Pump-push-probe experiments distinguish interfacial from bulk recombination by selectively photoexciting trapped populations[^45].

## **5. Technological Implications for Solar Energy Harvesting**

### **5.1 Hot Carrier Solar Cells**

The observation of slowed hot carrier cooling in perovskites (< 10 ps vs. < 1 ps in GaAs) reignited interest in hot carrier photovoltaics[^46]. By extracting carriers before thermalization, theoretical detailed-balance efficiencies exceed 66% under 1-sun illumination—surpassing the Shockley-Queisser limit[^47].

Challenges include:
- Maintaining slow cooling at practical carrier densities (> 10¹⁸ cm⁻³)
- Engineering selective energy contacts for hot carrier extraction
- Minimizing thermalization losses in polycrystalline grain boundaries

Competitive cooling pathways—Auger recombination and phonon-assisted relaxation—accelerate at high intensities. Recent intensity-dependent TA studies suggest the hot phonon bottleneck persists to ~10¹⁸ cm⁻³, encouraging for concentrator applications[^48].

### **5.2 Defect Passivation Strategies**

Ultrafast spectroscopy provides feedback loops for device optimization:

| Passivation Approach | Spectroscopic Signature | Efficiency Improvement |
|---------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Lewis bases (thiourea, pyridine) | Reduced fast TA component (trapping) | +2–3% absolute PCE[^49] |
| 2D/3D heterostructures | Extended PL lifetime | Enhanced stability[^50] |
| Halide substitution (Cl, Br) | Cleaner bandedge dynamics | Higher voltage[^51] |

The correlation between ultrafast trap dynamics and steady-state device performance validates spectroscopy as a predictive tool.

### **5.3 Interface Engineering**

Time-resolved studies guide contact material selection:

- **TiO₂ vs. SnO₂**: SnO₂ shows faster electron extraction and reduced hysteresis[^52]
- **2D perovskite interlayers**: Spiro-OMeTAD/2D/3D stacks enhance hole extraction while blocking ions[^53]
- **Self-assembled monolayers**: Molecular dipoles tune interfacial energetics, accelerating charge transfer by up to 5×[^54]

Ultrafast THz measurements confirm that optimized interfaces yield the highest effective mobilities (~60 cm²/V·s in champion devices).

### **5.4 Stability and Degradation**

Film degradation manifests spectroscopically as:

- **PL blueshift**: Formation of lower-bandgap degradation products (PbI₂)[^55]
- **Accelerated trapping**: Increased sub-gap TA signal from metallic Pb⁰[^56]
- **Reduced conductivity**: THz mobility decrease under thermal/ moisture stress[^57]

Rapid screening protocols using TA/ TRPL lifetimes predict operational stability, accelerating material development cycles.

## **6. Challenges and Future Directions**

### **6.1 Experimental Challenges**

Despite rich insights, several technical hurdles persist:

**Scattering in polycrystalline films**: Textured surfaces produce probe pulse distortion; polarization-dependent studies require careful normalization[^58].

**Carrier density regimes**: Different dynamics dominate under 1-sun (10¹⁵ cm⁻³) vs. laser-excitation (10¹⁸ cm⁻³) conditions; extrapolation requires care[^59].

**Ion migration**: Under electric fields and illumination, mobile ions screen internal fields, complicating time-resolved measurements of thin-film devices[^60].

**Defining "intrinsic" dynamics**: Sample-to-sample variation (deposition methods, annealing, stoichiometry) produces conflicting literature reports; standardized protocols are needed[^61].

### **6.2 Emerging Techniques**

Next-generation ultrafast methods promise deeper insights:

- **Attosecond spectroscopy**: Probing coherent electron dynamics within the optical cycle
- **Multimodal correlative imaging**: Combining TA, Raman, and electron microscopy on identical regions
- **Machine learning analysis**: Neural networks disentangling complex multi-exponential decays from sparse data[^62]
- **Operando ultrafast spectroscopy**: Probing working devices under bias and illumination[^63]

### **6.3 Theoretical Integration**

Connecting spectroscopic observables to atomistic mechanisms requires:

- **Non-adiabatic molecular dynamics**: Simulating coupled electron-nuclear motion on ultrafast timescales[^64]
- **Open quantum systems**: Treating polaron formation beyond the Born-Oppenheimer approximation
- **Multiscale modeling**: Linking femtosecond electronic dynamics to mesoscopic transport

Collaboration between spectroscopists, materials scientists, and theorists remains essential.

## **7. Conclusion**

Ultrafast laser spectroscopy has fundamentally transformed understanding of carrier dynamics in polycrystalline halide perovskites. From the initial moments of hot carrier generation through long-range transport and eventual recombination, pump-probe techniques chronicle the complete lifecycle of photoexcitations with exquisite temporal precision.

Key revelations include the importance of polaronic transport, the deleterious role of grain boundaries, the promise (and challenges) of hot carrier harvesting, and the criticality of interface engineering. These molecular-level insights directly inform device optimization strategies—passivation protocols, contact selection, and composition tuning all benefit from ultrafast spectroscopic feedback.

As the field matures, integration of multiple techniques (TA + TRTS + microscopy), multimodal correlative imaging, and machine learning analysis will drive the next wave of discovery. The continued synergy between ultrafast spectroscopy and perovskite photovoltaics promises to expedite the transition from laboratory curiosity to commercial energy technology, illuminating the path toward sustainable solar power for a decarbonized future.
