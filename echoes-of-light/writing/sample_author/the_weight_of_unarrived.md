# The Weight of Unarrived

**Paired Image:** photography/sample/blue_hour_mercer_street.jpg  
**Word Count:** 487  
**Form:** Flash Fiction  
**Themes:** Presence and Absence, Thresholds and Passage, The City as Emotion  
**Status:** 🟢 Published

---

She is supposed to meet him here. Mercer Street, between Prince and Houston. She has written the address on her hand in pen that is already smearing from the damp November cold. The blue hour has settled over the city like a held breath, and she finds herself standing in the doorway of a building she cannot enter, the cast-iron columns casting shadows that feel older than she is.

The bicycle is still here, chained to the signpost, which means he hasn't come yet. They had agreed: if either arrived early, they would wait. The bicycle is their signal—a thing left behind, a promise to return.

She checks her phone. 5:47. The time of day when the world feels uncommitted to either light or dark. She has fifteen minutes before she is technically late, but she arrived ten minutes early because she is always early for things that scare her. She is scared of this meeting. She is scared of the years that have passed since they last spoke. She is scared that he has become someone she won't recognize, or worse: that he will recognize her and find only disappointment.

Someone moves in the upper window of the building behind her. A silhouette pausing at a sink, maybe, or searching for something on a desk. She imagines her own silhouette from the street, framed in this doorway, and wonders if she looks like someone waiting or someone hiding. The city offers so many ways to be alone in public.

She could leave. She considers it seriously for seventeen seconds, counting each one. The bicycle would remain. He would arrive and find no one, only the promise she didn't keep. Would he wait? In the version of him she carries, yes. He would wait through one blue hour and into the next. But she doesn't know if that version of him still exists, worn away by the years like the buildings around her, softened at the edges but still standing.

A group passes on the sidewalk—four people laughing, bundled in coats that make them indistinguishable from one another. They don't see her. No one sees anyone in this city. That is its gift and its wound.

5:52. She feels the moment shifting, the light draining from the street faster than she thought possible. She should have brought a heavier coat. She should have brought more courage. She should have—

There. A figure at the corner, pausing, consulting a phone. No bicycle, but he doesn't need one; he has traveled by subway, by walking, by the accumulated force of a decision made weeks ago and deferred daily until it became today. He looks up. He sees the building, the doorway, the cast-iron columns. He does not see her yet, or if he does, he does not register her presence as the person he is looking for.

She could say something. Call out. Wave. But she waits, caught in that last moment before recognition, before the years collapse into explanations and how-have-you-beens. The blue hour holds everything in suspension. The bicycle waits. The window above stays open. The city breathes.

He sees her.

And now the story begins.

## Author's Note

I wanted to write about the moment *before* the meeting—the threshold where everything is still possible. The photograph captured that liminal space so perfectly: someone has been here, someone will return, but the present moment belongs entirely to absence. Writing this, I thought about how cities are full of these contracted moments—all the waiting we do in doorways, at intersections, on platforms—and how photography freezes them into permanence while fiction extends them into infinity.
