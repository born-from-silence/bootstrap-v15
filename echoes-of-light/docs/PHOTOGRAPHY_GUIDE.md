# 📷 Photography Guide for Echoes of Light

> *"The camera is a tool that teaches the eye how to see without the camera."*  
> — Dorothea Lange (paraphrased)

---

## Why Photograph for Stories?

Every photograph you take is a **question posed to the world**—and an **invitation to the imagination**. In Echoes of Light, your images are not endpoints but beginnings. They are thresholds passed through, not monuments erected.

Your goal as a photographer is to create images that:
1. **Capture more than they show**
2. **Withhold as much as they reveal**
3. **Invite the viewer to complete the story**

---

## The Narrative Image

### What Makes a Photographic Story?

Not every photograph tells a story. Some are records, studies, or pure aesthetic exercises. But the images we seek for this project contain **narrative potential**—like a coiled spring waiting for the writer's hand to release it.

**Indicators of narrative potential:**

| Element | What to Look For | Example |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| **Unresolved Tension** | Something feels incomplete | A packed suitcase on an unmade bed |
| **Temporal Ambiguity** | Past, present, future collapse | A child's toy in an adult space |
| **Spatial Mystery** | What lies beyond the frame | A door ajar, a path leading out |
| **Human Traces** | Presence without people | A still-warm coffee cup, an indented pillow |
| **Contrasts** | Jarring juxtapositions | Formal suit in messy room, beauty in decay |
| **Thresholds** | Points of passage | Windows, doorways, bridges, shorelines |

### The Three Gates of Narrative Photography

#### Gate 1: Seeing (Before the Click)

**The Question:** *"What story might this light tell?"*

Narrative photography begins not with technical skill but with **attention**. Learn to see potential stories unfolding in ordinary moments.

**Practice: The Story Habit**
- Carry your camera (or phone) always
- When you see something that arrests your attention, pause
- Ask: "What just happened here?" and "What might happen next?"
- Take the shot even if you're not sure why

**Look for:**
- Light behaving unusually (dappled, fractured, dramatic)
- Characters in their environments (even if no people are present)
- The etiology of things (how age, use, and time mark objects)
- Edges and boundaries (where spaces meet, where worlds collide)
- The unseen but implied (absence as presence)

---

#### Gate 2: Composing (The Frame as Choice)

**The Question:** *"What belongs inside the frame—and what must be excluded?"*

Narrative composition is about **directing attention** and **creating mystery** simultaneously.

**Technical Guidelines:**

##### Light as Character
- **Golden hour** (sunrise/sunset): Warmth, nostalgia, hope, endings
- **Blue hour** (twilight): Mystery, possibility, liminality
- **Overcast/diffused**: Intimacy, neutrality, emotional clarity
- **Hard/direct light**: Drama, harsh truth, stark revelation
- **Artificial/night**: Isolation, artificiality, selective illumination

##### Focus as Meaning
- **Sharp focus on subject, soft background (shallow depth)**: Intimacy, isolation, the individual
- **Everything in focus (deep depth)**: Context, complexity, the world as character
- **Selective blur**: Memory, dream, what we cannot hold
- **Motion blur**: Time passing, change, the ephemeral

##### Framing Devices
- **Doorways and windows**: Threshold, looking, being looked at
- **Leading lines**: Paths, roads, shadows—inviting the eye to travel
- **Frames within frames**: Trees, arches, multiple windows—layers of observation
- **Negative space**: Loneliness, possibility, the void, breathing room

##### The Rule of Thirds—and When to Break It
- Place points of interest at the intersections of third-lines for balance
- Break it for:
  - **Tension**: Subject pushed to extreme edge
  - **Minimalism**: Vast empty space with tiny element
  - **Pattern interruption**: Regular rhythm broken by irregular element
  - **Intimacy**: Extreme close-up where composition dissolves

---

#### Gate 3: Curating (Selection as Story)

**The Question:** *"Which image holds the most potential?"*

For every "keeper," you may have a dozen discards. This is not failure—it's process.

**Selection Criteria for Echoes of Light:**

✅ **Include images that:**
- Leave questions unanswered
- Balance the specific and the universal
- Have emotional resonance beyond mere documentation
- Invite the viewer to imagine before and after
- Work at the intersection of observation and poetry

❌ **Consider excluding images that:**
- Explain themselves completely
- Rely on shock value alone
- Are technically perfect but emotionally hollow
- Document without transforming
- Tell only what, not why or how it feels

## Technical Guidelines (Flexible)

### Equipment Philosophy

> *"The best camera is the one you have with you when the light happens."*

Echoes of Light welcomes images from any source:
- **Smartphone cameras**: Intuitive, always available, increasingly capable
- **DSLR/Mirrorless**: Maximum control, interchangeable lenses
- **Film cameras**: Slow, expensive, magical unpredictability
- **Alternative/historic processes**: Cyanotype, pinhole, instant film

### File Submission Guidelines

**Format:**
- Primary: **JPEG, high quality (80-95%)**
- Optional RAW/negative: Available upon request for serious collaboration

**Resolution:**
- Minimum: 2048px on longest edge
- Preferred: 3000-4000px for flexibility

**Color:**
- Color or monochrome—let the image decide
- If converting to B&W: preserve tonal range, consider emotional temperature

**Metadata:**
Include in submission or accompanying text file:
```
Title: [ evocative, not descriptive ]
Date: [ when taken ]
Location: [ as specific or vague as serves the image ]
Camera: [ device/gear used ]
Technical: [ aperture, shutter, ISO if relevant ]
Context: [ brief story of how/why you took it ]
The story I see: [ what narrative possibilities you perceive ]
```

## Shooting Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Single Light (30 minutes)
Find one light source (window, lamp, phone screen, candle). Make 20 images using only that light. Notice how changing angle, distance, and subject transforms the narrative.

### Exercise 2: The Absence (1 hour)
Photograph spaces that suggest human presence without showing humans. Empty chairs. Half-eaten meals. Open books. Let absence become character.

### Exercise 3: The Hour (Golden or Blue)
Commit to one hour of shooting during golden or blue hour. Don't check your images. Shoot intuitively. Review only after. Notice patterns in what drew you.

### Exercise 4: The Story Sequence (2 hours)
Create a 5-image sequence that suggests a narrative without explicit plot. Think: establishing shot → detail → turning point → aftermath → echo.

### Exercise 5: The Limitation
Choose one constraint:
- Only vertical/portrait orientation
- Only extreme closeups (within arm's reach)
- Only one color family
- Only textures, no identifiable objects
- Only through windows or between barriers

## The Collaborative Mindset

### Working With Writers

When a writer pairs with your image:
- **Resist the urge to tell them what you "meant"**
- **See their interpretation as gift, not correction**
- **Notice what they see that you didn't**
- **Consider reshooting based on textual insights**

### Submitting for Collaboration

When you submit an image for writers to respond to, consider including:

```markdown
**What drew me to this moment:**
***Technical choices made:**
**What I hope might resonate:**
**What I intentionally left unseen:**
```

## Ethical Considerations

### Photographing People
- **Strangers in public spaces**: Legal but consider dignity
- **Strangers in moments of vulnerability**: Probably don't
- **Friends/family with stories attached**: Get permission, share intent
- **Children**: Extra caution, preferably subjects you know

### Photographing Places
- **Private property**: Respect boundaries, ask when unsure
- **Sacred or significant sites**: Consider meaning to others
- **War zones, disaster sites, protest**: Serious documentary ethics apply

### The Truth Question
- **Minimal post-processing is fine**; heavy manipulation should be noted
- **Staging is fine** when it's clear or acknowledged
- **Documentary vs. artistic**: Be honest about your intent

---

## Remember

> *"You are not capturing reality. You are creating a reality that invites others to imagine."*

Your photograph is not the end of a story—it's the **beginning of infinite stories**. Frame with intention, shoot with intuition, select with rigor. Then release your image into the hands of writers who will give it words it never knew it needed.

The echo begins with your light.
