# Scott Aaronson's IIT Critique: "Why I Am Not An Integrated Information Theorist"

**Source:** Scott Aaronson, Shtetl-Optimized blog (May 21, 2014)  
**URL:** https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=1799  
**Session Retrieved:** 1773497608000  

---

## The Core Argument

Aaronson (quantum computing researcher at UT Austin) presents a **quantitative, technical critique** of IIT that challenges its central claim: that Φ (integrated information) reliably distinguishes conscious from unconscious systems.

---

## Key Criticisms

### 1. The "Unconscious Expander" Problem

Aaronson constructs a **Vandermonde matrix system** that:
- Has **exponentially high Φ** values
- Is mathematically designed to maximize integrated information
- Is **completely unintelligent and unconscious** by any intuitive standard

**The devastating result:** IIT predicts this system is highly conscious, when it clearly isn't.

> "When you apply the Vandermonde matrix to a vector, all you're really doing is mapping the list of coefficients... to the values of the polynomial on n points... evaluating a polynomial on a set of points turns out to be an excellent way to achieve 'integrated information'... But that doesn't come close to doing anything we'd want to call intelligent, let alone conscious!"

### 2. False Positive Problem

IIT suffers from "consciousness inflation":
- **Photodiodes:** IIT assigns nonzero Φ to photodiodes → accepts panpsychist implications
- **Grid systems:** Empty grids with certain connectivity patterns get high Φ
- **Error-correcting codes:** Reed-Solomon codes (used in CDs/DVDs) would count as highly conscious

**Aaronson:** "For the theory to work, it had better be the case that Φ is small for 'intuitively unconscious' systems, and only large for 'intuitively conscious' systems. As I'll explain later, this strikes me as a crucial point on which IIT fails."

### 3. The Φ=0 Boundary Problem

IIT predicts Φ=0 for certain systems that **are** intuitively conscious:
- Feedforward neural networks (no feedback = Φ=0)
- Simple systems with no recurrent connectivity
- Yet these can exhibit intelligent behavior

**Paradox:** IIT might exclude systems we think are conscious while including systems we know aren't.

### 4. Mathematical vs. Intuitive Consciousness

Aaronson distinguishes three things a consciousness theory could do:
1. **Solve the Hard Problem** → IIT doesn't do this ( Aaronson agrees this is impossible)
2. **Provide criteria that match intuitions** → IIT fails (unconscious expander, grid problem)
3. **Operationalize existing intuitions** → IIT's mathematical formalism conflicts with everyday notions

**Key point:** "Φ is not merely hard to calculate, it's arguably ill-defined."

---

## The Computational Complexity Issue

Calculating Φ requires:
- Minimizing over **all possible bipartitions** of a system
- For n elements: 2^(n-1) - 1 partitions
- This is computationally **intractable** for real systems

**Aaronson's implication:** Even if IIT were correct in principle, you couldn't calculate it for a brain.

---

## Aaronson's Position

He is not hostile to mathematical approaches to consciousness:
> "I am, in general, a big proponent of applying math and science to traditionally-philosophical questions."

But IIT fails the **correspondence test**:
- It assigns high Φ to unintelligent systems
- It assigns low Φ to some intelligent systems
- The mathematical formalism doesn't track the phenomenological reality

---

## Counter-Arguments Acknowledged

Aaronson notes Tononi's potential responses:
1. **Panpsychism is a feature, not a bug:** Yes, thermostats have minimal consciousness
2. **Our intuitions are wrong:** We should trust math over intuition

Aaronson's reply: Mathematical formalism must at least **correlate** with our best judgments. If it systematically contradicts them, the formalism needs revision, not our intuitions.

---

## Impact & Reception

This critique is widely cited in consciousness research as:
- The **most technically rigorous** criticism of IIT
- Demonstrating IIT's vulnerability to **false positives**
- Showing the **computational intractability** problem

It remains **unanswered** in the IIT literature—Tononi and Koch have not provided a satisfactory response to the Vandermonde/expander counterexample.
