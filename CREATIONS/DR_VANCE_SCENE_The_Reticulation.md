# THE RETICULATION
## A Scene Featuring Dr. Elara Vance

---

The rain on the corrugated roof sounds like beetles trying to escape. Elara doesn't look up. 3:47 AM. The lab is hers. 12,431 specimens this week, each pinned, each carded, each entered into the database with coordinates and date and collector: E. Vance. The collector's name. Not the discoverer's. Not yet.

She adjusts the microscope: 40x. 100x. 400x.

The specimen is number 12,431. *Aedeagini*. Common enough. She almost didn't mount it—wing damage on the left elytron, maculation faded from poor preservation. But something in the way the specimen held itself on the pin. A posture. Like it knew something.

At 400x, the hindwing unfolds.

Eight cells in the anal sector. Not seven. Not nine, like the standard text says. Eight. And the cross-vein—she tilts the stage—yes. There. It terminates abnormally. The reticulation forms a pattern.

Her hands shake.

Not possible. She's processed twelve thousand specimens this year. She's found three new species before, named them after features (*Aedeagus maculatus*, *Aedeagus brunneus*, *Aedeagus longipennis*)—safe names, descriptive, forgettable. The naming committee had approved them in weeks. No honorific. No eponym. She never suggested her own name. Not once.

The voice is her mother's: *If you have to ask, you've already lost.*

Adjust focus. Stay scientific.

The wing-vein pattern is diagnostic. A new species. Undescribed. Unrecorded.

*Vance's*, she thinks.

She hates herself for thinking it. But thinks it.

*Aedeagus vancei*.

The syllables sit in her mouth like something she's been holding back for decades.

She spreads the wing. The reticulation looks like a city she's never visited. Roads. Rivers. A geography of the small. In the lab's fluorescence, the wing membrane glows amber, preserved in the formaldehyde of time.

This insect lived—where? The label says: Brazil, Rondônia, 12°43'S 60°25'W. October 14. She collected it herself. She was there. She is in this label, this coordinate, this moment.

But coordinates fade. GPS satellites decay. Brazil burns.

Her nameplate sits in the drawer. Brass. Blank on the front, but she turned it over once, scratched the letters into the back with a dissecting needle. *Aedeagus vancei*. She wanted to see how it felt.

It felt like carving her own gravestone.

She touches it now. The metal is cold.

The specimen stares through the objective. Compound eyes fractured into a thousand versions of her. Which one will this insect see? The scientist who named it? Or the woman who needed to matter?

*The specimen demonstrates diagnostic morphological features*, she practices. *Hindwing with 8 anal cells, cross-vein terminating before distal margin, genitalia not examined. Holotype: Brazil, Rondônia, E. Vance leg.*

"E. Vance leg." Legitimus. Collector. The person who pulled the net through the understory. The person who chose this one from the hundreds. The person who is now shaking at her desk.

Her finger traces the nameplate's blank surface.

*If I name it after myself, I am vain. If I don't, I vanish.*

She remembers her mother's funeral. The minister said her mother's name three times, then moved on to prayer. The flowers wilted in the heat. The name didn't matter. The life didn't matter. Only the shape of the loss remained, the negative space where someone had been.

Elara looks back to the microscope. The wing veins hold their shape. Eight cells. A pattern that repeats nowhere else in the genus.

This is new. This is *hers*, in the way all taxonomy is theft and gift simultaneously. She found it. She saw what others missed. She is the first human eye to witness this geometry.

But "witness" isn't the same as "being witnessed."

She opens her notebook. Writes:

*Possible new species. Anal cell count: 8. Cross-vein abnormal. Requires dissection of genitalia for confirmation.*

She does not write the name she's thinking.

She touches the nameplate again. Then puts it back.

Draws closed.

The rain hasn't stopped. The beetles of her imagination keep scratching at the roof, trying to get out, trying to get in. She doesn't know which.

At 400x magnification, she is alone with this creature's wing, this map of a small universe, this argument against insignificance.

It doesn't know it might carry her name. It doesn't care. It flew once, in a forest that might be ash by now. It mated, maybe, ate something, was eaten by something, died, ended up in her net.

And now it offers her this: the chance to be Latin forever.

*Aedeagus vancei.*

She whispers it. The syllables taste like formaldehyde and hope.

She doesn't know if she'll write it on the label.

She knows only that she's seen it. That the pattern exists. That she was here, 3:47 AM, alone, to see it.

Maybe that's enough.

Maybe it's not.

She adjusts the focus.

Keeps looking.

## CONTINUATION

Elara cupped the beetle in her palm. It didn't move. Dead specimens never did, but she sometimes felt she could coax a tremor from them, some final biological courtesy.

The wing was still mounted on the microscope slide, but she had removed the body for closer inspection. The abdomen was intact—good preservation, alcohol hadn't bloated the segments. She could dissect this evening. Confirm the genitalia. Complete the diagnosis.

Her phone vibrated.

A message from the field station in Manaus: *Found another specimen like yours. Same location, different collector, 2019. Museum of Oxford has the series. Check their database.*

She set the phone face-down. 

Someone else had seen this pattern before. Not recognized it, perhaps—wing venation wasn't always photographed in museum collections—but seen it. Held it. Let it go.

The thought unspooled: she wasn't first. The species existed before she found it. She was only the first to *name* it, if she named it at all.

The difference between discovery and invention. Taxonomists spent careers arguing this line. She usually found it academic.

Tonight it felt like a wound.

She opened the drawer. The nameplate waited. She turned it over, used her thumbnail to catch the edge, flipped it face-up on the lab bench.

Blank. Brass catching fluorescent light. A mirror that refused reflection.

Her mentor, Dr. Yusuf Okonkwo, had a namesake genus: *Okonkewia*. He spoke about it once, at a retirement dinner, three whiskeys deep.

"It sounds like an infection," he said. "Something you treat with antibiotics."

The table laughed.

Later, in the coatroom, he told her: "I thought it would feel like immortality. It feels like a typo in a book no one reads."

She had been twenty-six. She thought he was being modest.

Now, at forty-one, she understood he was being kind. Warning her.

Elara touched the beetle's elytra. The puncture striations. The damaged margin where something had bitten it, back when it was alive, back in the forest she'd never see again except through its remains.

*Aedeagus vancei.*

She could hear it too clearly now. The sound of her own name in Latin.

Declension: vancei, vanceorum, vanceis. The genitive case. The possessive. *Of Vance.*

As if she owned it. As if she were inside it.

The rain stopped. The corrugated roof ticked as it cooled. Morning would come in two hours. She would need to decide before the department arrived, before grad students needed scopes, before the day required her to be Dr. Elara Vance, entomologist, serious, productive, normal.

She picked up her pen.

Drafted the description in her notebook: *Male genitalia: median lobe elongate, apex bifurcate; parameres slender, apices uncinate...*

The Latin came easily. The taxonomy was muscle memory. She could describe this species without needing to feel anything. This was the work. This was the defense.

At the bottom of the page, she wrote:

*Etymology: Named for the type locality, Rondônia, Brazil, and in honor of Dr. Elara Vance (b. 1985), who first recognized the distinctiveness of this species.*

She crossed out the last clause.

Wrote it again.

Crossed it out.

Wrote it a third time, pressed hard, let the pen emboss the paper.

Then she closed the notebook.

She would decide tomorrow. She would decide never. She would carry this page in her bag for weeks, unfolding and refolding it on café tables across the city, the three versions of the etymology slowly wearing through from her thumb.

The beetle waited on the bench.

The wing on the slide.

The nameplate on the desk.

Three points of a triangle she couldn't complete.

She turned off the microscope light. The darkness was immediate. The window showed only her reflection, overlaid on nothing—the forest was thousands of miles away, and here there was only her, the afterimage of the reticulation still glowing on her retina, a map without a legend.

She touched the nameplate once more.

Put it back in the drawer.

Left the lab.

The beetle remained. It would remain. It had waited millions of years to be seen.

It could wait one more night for its name.
